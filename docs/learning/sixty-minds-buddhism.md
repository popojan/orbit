# 六十心 (Liù Shí Xīn) — The Sixty Minds

**Source:** 大日經 (Dà Rì Jīng) — Mahāvairocana Sūtra, Chapter on Dwelling in Mind (住心品)
**Commentary:** 大日經疏 (Dà Rì Jīng Shū) — Commentary on the Mahāvairocana Sūtra

---

## The Character 心 (Xīn)

The character 心 is a pictogram of the heart organ:

```
  ♡  →  心
```

In Chinese (and Buddhist) usage, 心 means both:

| Meaning | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Heart** (organ) | 心臟 (xīnzàng) |
| **Mind/consciousness** | 心理 (xīnlǐ) = psychology |
| **Center of feeling** | 開心 (kāixīn) = happy |

In Buddhism, 心 translates Sanskrit **citta** (mind/consciousness). Unlike Western tradition which separates "heart" (emotions) from "mind" (reason), Chinese/Buddhist thought unifies them in 心.

Therefore 六十心 = "Sixty Hearts" = "Sixty Minds" — both translations are valid.

---

## Overview

The Sixty Minds (六十心) represent mental states that may arise during yogic practice. According to the commentary, these are "mental tendencies that sometimes are predominant in the practitioner's inherent nature."

This classification comes from Chinese Esoteric Buddhism (密教, Mìjiào) and is distinct from:
- Theravada's 52 cetasikas (mental factors)
- Yogachara's 51 mental factors
- Tibetan Buddhism's 80 indicative conceptions

## The Dancing Mind (舞心) — #33

**Chinese:** 舞心 (wǔ xīn)
**Literal:** "Dancing mind" or "mind of dance"

**Description from commentary:**
> "Dancing Mind practices such dharma, thinking, 'I will ascend through various divine transformations.'"

This mental state represents the aspiration to transcend ordinary existence through spiritual practice, visualizing oneself rising through successive levels of divine transformation — like a dancer moving through increasingly sublime movements.

---

## Context in Practice

These 60 minds are not prescriptive (good vs. bad) but descriptive — they represent mental patterns that naturally arise during meditation and yogic practice. The practitioner learns to recognize these states and understand their nature.

The commentary notes these represent "mental tendencies that sometimes are predominant in the practitioner's inherent nature."
